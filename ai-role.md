# The Role of AI in Treatment Decisions

Sid presented at the OpenAI Forum with a talk titled "Terminal to Turnaround: How GitLab's Co-Founder Leveraged ChatGPT in His Cancer Fight." AI tools were integral to his founder-mode approach.

---

## How AI Was Used

### 1. Literature Review and Synthesis

With 1,000+ page health notes and hundreds of research papers relevant to osteosarcoma treatment, AI served as a research assistant:
- Summarizing relevant papers
- Identifying potential treatment options from emerging research
- Cross-referencing his specific mutation profile against published case reports
- Tracking clinical trials relevant to his tumor biology

### 2. Decision Support

Sid used balanced scorecards for major treatment decisions (documented in his Substack podcast "Weighing the good story factor"). AI likely assisted in:
- Structuring decision matrices
- Evaluating trade-offs between treatment options
- Identifying risks and contraindications
- Modeling treatment combinations

### 3. Data Interpretation

With multi-modal diagnostic data (genomics, transcriptomics, imaging, ctDNA), AI helped:
- Interpret complex genomic reports from 5+ sequencing providers
- Identify patterns across scRNA-seq timepoints
- Cross-reference GSEA results with treatment response
- Synthesize flow cytometry trends with treatment timeline

### 4. Communication

As a non-physician managing a complex care team across multiple institutions, AI assisted in:
- Preparing questions for specialist consultations
- Understanding medical terminology and pathology reports
- Drafting treatment rationale documents
- Communicating with research collaborators

## The Balanced Scorecard Framework

From Sid's Substack, he applies business strategy tools to healthcare decisions:

**Process**:
1. Identify key decision pillars (e.g., efficacy, toxicity, availability, evidence quality)
2. Define desired outcomes for each pillar
3. Assign weighted importance percentages (must total 100%)
4. Score each treatment option against each pillar
5. Calculate weighted scores
6. Make decision based on data, not emotion or tradition

This is a systematic approach to decisions that most patients make intuitively or defer entirely to physicians.

---

## AI as Equalizer

The information asymmetry between patients and physicians is enormous. AI tools partially close this gap:

| Without AI | With AI |
|------------|---------|
| Physician interprets, patient trusts | Patient can independently verify and question |
| Research locked behind jargon and paywalls | AI translates and synthesizes |
| Treatment options limited to physician's knowledge | AI surfaces global options including expanded access |
| Decision-making is physician-led | Patient can participate meaningfully |

---

## Limitations

AI was a tool, not a decision-maker. Key limitations:
- Cannot replace clinical judgment for drug interactions and toxicity
- May hallucinate or cite retracted studies
- Cannot access real-time patient data without integration
- Does not replace the need for expert physicians and researchers

Sid's team included a dedicated care coordinator (Jacob Stern), clinical advisory board, and scientific advisory board. AI augmented, not replaced, human expertise.

---

## Implications for Scaling

AI-assisted patient decision-making is the most scalable part of the founder-mode model:

- **Low cost**: ChatGPT/Claude subscriptions vs. concierge medical services
- **Always available**: No scheduling required
- **Improves over time**: Models get better at medical reasoning
- **Democratizing**: Available to any patient, not just billionaires

The Elliot Hershberg article envisions AI agents that:
1. Analyze medical history upon early cancer detection
2. Order refined diagnostics automatically
3. Generate comprehensive bioinformatics reports
4. Recommend personalized treatment combinations
5. Monitor response in real-time via ctDNA

Estimated future cost for AI-guided personalized oncology: ~$175,000 (vs. $250,000+ for current standard pancreatic cancer treatment).
